##### Abstract

The archives of ancient Ebla provide abundant information on family connections within the city. Individuals are primarily identified through their patronymics, indicating their father's name, while other kinship terms are less common. This scribal practice has resulted in extensive knowledge of the important families associated with royal Palace G and the city, although some primary kin relationships, such as parents, spouses, children, and siblings, can only be inferred, along with secondary kinship ties. Kinship terms describe the relationships established through marriage or descent, positioning individuals within their kin groups. Given the significance of the royal family and the families of ministers in Ebla's social structure, understanding an individual's role and functions necessitates consideration of their connections to extended kin groups. These families held political power and governed vast territories, and their interconnections are crucial to Eblaite relative chronology. Evaluating an individual's position within these larger families is paramount to comprehending their role in the broader social context. Although royal sons (dumu-nita en) frequently appear in the administrative records, their status requires further investigation. This study aims to examine the functions and roles of the royal sons in Ebla during the archival period, taking into account their kinship ties with other members of the royal family.
